Derivation of the Navier–Stokes equations

Posted on 2019-06-26 Edited on 2020-12-25

Assumption

In this post, we apply some assumptions into our derivation process:

  • Newtonian fluid
  • Incompressible flow

Reynolds Transport Theorem

Material Volume and Control Volume moving over time

Here, we obtain the Reynolds Transport Theorem which provides the conversion formulation between Lagrangian description (LHS) and Eulerian description (RHS).

\[ \eqalign{\frac{D}{Dt}\int\limits{\textbf{MV}(t)}\textbf{F}(\vec{\mathrm{r}},t)\mathrm{d}\Omega=\frac{d}{dt}\int\limits{\textbf{CV}(t)}\textbf{F}(\vec{\mathrm{r}},t) \ \mathrm{d}\Omega + \oint\limits{\textbf{CS}(t)}\textbf{F}(\vec{\mathrm{r}},t)\vec{V}\cdot \vec{n}\ \mathrm{d}S} \]

Where the subscript MV indicates material volume, CV indicates control volume, and CS is control surface.

Further apply divergence theorem ( \(\int\vec{\mathrm{A}} \cdot \vec{n} \ \mathrm{d}S=\int\nabla \cdot \vec{\mathrm{A}} \ \mathrm{d}\Omega\) ) to convert surface integrals in above equation into volume integrals. This gives us an equation as follow.

\[ \eqalign{\frac{D}{Dt}\int\limits{\textbf{MV}} \ \textbf{F}\mathrm{d}\Omega=\frac{d}{dt}\int\limits{\textbf{CV}} \ \textbf{F} \ \mathrm{d}\Omega + \int\limits{ \textbf{CV}} \ \nabla\cdot ( \textbf{F} \vec{V})\ \mathrm{d} \Omega} \]

The terms in Eulerian description (RHS) become volume integrals now. Let \(\textbf{F} = \rho \phi\), then we have,

\[ \eqalign{ \frac{D}{Dt}\int\limits{\textbf{MV}}\rho \ \phi\ \mathrm{d}\Omega=\frac{d}{dt}\int\limits{\textbf{CV}} \rho \ \phi\ \mathrm{d}\Omega + \int\limits{\textbf{CV}}\nabla\cdot ( \rho \ \phi \vec{V})\ \mathrm{d}\Omega } \]

When we further consider the arbitrary (shape and size) but fixed control volume (independent of time), we have the constraint:

\[ \eqalign{ \frac{d}{dt}\int\limits{\textbf{CV}} \textbf{F}\mathrm{d}\Omega = \int\limits{\textbf{CV}}\frac{\partial F}{\partial t}\mathrm{d}\Omega } \]

Take the constraint into the equation above gives us:

\[ \eqalign{\frac{D}{Dt}\int\limits_{\textbf{MV}}\textbf{F}\mathrm{d}\Omega = \int\limits_{\textbf{CV}}\left[ \frac{\partial \textbf{F}}{\partial t}+\nabla \cdot(\textbf{F}\vec{V})\right]\mathrm{d}\Omega} \] because, \[ \eqalign{ \nabla\cdot(\textbf{F}\vec{V})=\vec{V}\cdot\nabla \textbf{F}+\textbf{F}\nabla\cdot\vec{V}} \] therefore, \[ \eqalign{ \frac{\partial \textbf{F}}{\partial t}+\nabla \cdot(\textbf{F}\vec{V})= \left(\frac{\partial \textbf{F}}{\partial t}+\vec{V}\cdot\nabla \textbf{F}\right)+\textbf{F}\nabla\cdot\vec{V}=\frac{D\textbf{F}}{Dt}+\textbf{F}\nabla\cdot\vec{V}} \] finally, \[ \eqalign{ \frac{D}{Dt}\int\limits_{\textbf{MV}}\textbf{F}\mathrm{d}\Omega = \int\limits_{\textbf{CV}}\frac{D\textbf{F}}{Dt}\mathrm{d}\Omega+\int\limits_{\textbf{CV}} \textbf{F}\nabla\cdot\vec{V}\mathrm{d}\Omega} \] and consider \(\textbf{F} = \rho \phi\), \[ \eqalign{ \frac{D}{Dt}\int\limits_{\textbf{MV}}\rho\phi\mathrm{d}\Omega = \int\limits_{\textbf{CV}}\frac{D(\rho\phi)}{Dt}\mathrm{d}\Omega+\int\limits_{\textbf{CV}} \rho\phi\nabla\cdot\vec{V}\mathrm{d}\Omega } \]

Accordingly, we obtain the form of Reynolds Transport Theorem within the fixed control volume. 

Conservation of Mass

Let \(\phi=1\), the mass \(m\) can be represented as

\[ \eqalign{ m =\int\limits{\textbf{MV}}\rho\ \mathrm{d}\Omega } \]

In the Lagrangian description, the equation for conservation of mass is written as,

\[ \eqalign{ \frac{Dm}{Dt} =\frac{D}{Dt}\int\limits{\textbf{MV}}\rho\ \mathrm{d}\Omega=0 \ . } \]

For arbitrary, but fixed control volume, we have the following equation.

\[ \eqalign{ \frac{D}{Dt}\int\limits_{\textbf{MV}}\rho \mathrm{d}\Omega& =\frac{d}{dt}\int\limits_{\textbf{CV}} \rho \mathrm{d}\Omega + \int\limits_{\textbf{CV}} \nabla\cdot ( \rho \vec{V})\ \mathrm{d}\Omega\\ & =\int\limits_{\textbf{CV}} \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} \mathrm{d}\Omega + \int\limits_{\textbf{CV}} \nabla\cdot ( \rho \vec{V})\ \mathrm{d}\Omega\\ & =\int\limits_{\textbf{CV}} \left[\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} + \nabla\cdot ( \rho \vec{V})\ \right]\mathrm{d}\Omega =0 } \]

Now, we have the differential form of the continuity equation in eulerian description.

\[ \eqalign{ \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} + \nabla\cdot ( \rho \vec{V})=\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} + \rho( \nabla\cdot\vec{V} ) + \vec{V}\cdot ( \nabla\rho )=0 } \]

Incompressible flow

Consider the incompressible flow (density \(\rho\) of a certain fluid parcel does not change as a function of time) in our applications, we have the following equation.

\[ \eqalign{ \underset{ Lagrangian }{ \frac{D\rho}{Dt} } = \underset{ Eulerian }{ \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t}+\vec{V}\cdot\nabla\rho} =0} \]

Compare the difference between the differential form of continuity equation and equation of incompressible flow condition, we have:

\[ \eqalign{ \nabla \cdot \vec{V}=0 } \]

Here we obtain the continuity equation for the incompressible flow.

Conservation of Momentum

Let \(\phi=\vec{V}\), the momentum \(\vec{M}\) can be represented as

\[ \eqalign{ \vec{M} =\int\limits{\textbf{MV}}\rho \vec{V} \mathrm{d}\Omega } \]

In the Lagrangian description, the equation for conservation of momentum is written as,

\[ \eqalign{ \underset{Rate \ of \ change \ of \ momentum \ in \ \textbf{MV}}{\frac{D}{Dt}\int\limits{\textbf{MV}}\vec{V}\rho \ \mathrm{d}\Omega}=\underset{ Body \ force}{\int\limits{\textbf{MV}}\vec{f}\rho \ \mathrm{d}\Omega}+\underset{ Surface \ force}{\int\limits{MS}\overline{\overline{T}}\cdot \vec{n}\mathrm{d}S} } \]

Where \(\overline{\overline{T}}\cdot \vec{n} = \vec{n} \cdot \overline{\overline{T}}\) is surface force, \(\overline{\overline{T}}\) is the stress tensor, and \(\vec{f}\) indicates the body force.

Use Reynolds transport theorem to transfer the equation above into the following equation:

\[ \eqalign{ {\frac{d}{dt}\int\limits{\textbf{CV}}\vec{V}\rho \ \mathrm{d}\Omega}+\int\limits{\textbf{CS}} \vec{V} \rho \vec{V}\cdot \vec{n}\mathrm{d}S={\int\limits{\textbf{CV}}\vec{f}\rho \ \mathrm{d}\Omega}+{\int\limits{\textbf{CS}}\overline{\overline{T}}\cdot \vec{n}\mathrm{d}S} } \]

Again, we apply divergence theorem ( \(\int\vec{\mathrm{A}} \cdot \vec{n} \ \mathrm{d}S=\int\nabla \cdot \vec{\mathrm{A}} \ \mathrm{d}\Omega\) ) on both side of the equation.

\[ \eqalign{ {\frac{d}{dt}\int\limits{\textbf{CV}}\vec{V}\rho \ \mathrm{d}\Omega}+\int\limits{\textbf{CV}} \nabla\cdot(\vec{V} \rho \vec{V})\mathrm{d}\Omega={\int\limits{\textbf{CV}}\vec{f}\rho \ \mathrm{d}\Omega}+{\int\limits{\textbf{CV}}\nabla\cdot\overline{\overline{T}}\mathrm{d}\Omega}} \]

For arbitrary, but fixed control volume, we have the following equation.

\[ \eqalign{ {\int\limits\textbf\frac{\partial}{\partial t}(\rho\vec{V}) \ \mathrm{d}\Omega}+\int\limits{\textbf{CV}} \nabla\cdot(\rho\vec{V} \vec{V})\mathrm{d}\Omega={\int\limits{\textbf{CV}} \rho \vec{f} \ \mathrm{d}\Omega}+{\int\limits{\textbf{CV}}\nabla\cdot\overline{\overline{T}}\mathrm{d}\Omega}} \]

and thus,

\[ \eqalign{ \frac{\partial}{\partial t}(\rho\vec{V}) +\nabla\cdot(\rho\vec{V} \vec{V}) =\rho\vec{f} +\nabla\cdot\overline{\overline{T}} } \]

Now, we have the differential form of the momentum Equations in eulerian description (not restricted to Newtonian fluid).

\[ \eqalign{ \frac{\partial}{\partial t}(\rho\vec{V}) +\nabla\cdot(\rho\vec{V} \vec{V}) =\rho\vec{f} +\nabla\cdot\overline{\overline{T}} } \]

Newtonian fluid

The stress tensor can be expressed as the sum of two stress tensors, namely: the hydrostatic stress tensor and the deviatoric stress tensor. Thus, \(\overline{\overline{T}}\) is broken down into

\[ \eqalign{ \overline{\overline{T}} =T{ij}= -p\delta{ij}+\tau{ij} } \]

The second part is called the deviatoric stress tensor. For a Newtonian Fluid, the deviatoric stress tensor is proportional to the rate of deformation (the change in velocity in the directions of the stress).

\[ \eqalign{ \tau{ij} = \mu\left( \frac{\partial ui}{\partial xj}+\frac{\partial uj}{\partial xi}\right)+\lambda\frac{\partial uk}{\partial xk}\delta{ij} } \]

The total stress tensor \(\overline{\overline{T}}\) is therefore

\[ \eqalign{ T{ij} = \left(-p+\lambda\frac{\partial uk}{\partial xk}\right)\delta{ij}+\mu\left( \frac{\partial ui}{\partial xj}+\frac{\partial uj}{\partial xi}\right) } . \]

In the vector form

\[ \eqalign{ \overline{\overline{T}} = \left(-p+\lambda\nabla \cdot\vec{V}\right)\overline{\overline{I}}+2\mu\overline{\overline{D}} } , \]

where \(\overline{\overline{D}}=D{ij}= \frac{1}{2}\left( \frac{\partial ui}{\partial xj}+\frac{\partial uj}{\partial xi}\right)\).

Substituting this into the previous equation, we arrive at the momentum equation for a Newtonian fluid:

\[ \eqalign{ \frac{\partial}{\partial t}(\rho\vec{V}) +\nabla\cdot(\rho\vec{V} \vec{V}) =\rho\vec{f}-\nabla p+\left(\mu+\lambda\right)\nabla\left(\nabla \cdot \vec{V}\right)+\mu\nabla^2\vec{V} } \]

Incompressible flow

Consider the incompressible flow (\(\nabla \cdot \vec{V}=0\)) in our applications, we have the following equation.

\[ \eqalign{ \frac{\partial}{\partial t}(\rho\vec{V}) +\nabla\cdot(\rho\vec{V} \vec{V})=\rho\vec{f} -\nabla p +\mu\nabla^2\vec{V} } \]

Here we obtain the momentum equation for the incompressible flow.

Navier-Stokes Equations

Therefore, we have derived the Navier-Stokes Equations of an incompressible flow.

\[ \eqalign{ \nabla \cdot \vec{V}=0 }, \]

\[ \eqalign{ \frac{\partial}{\partial t}(\rho\vec{V}) +\nabla\cdot(\rho\vec{V} \vec{V}) =\rho\vec{f} -\nabla p +\mu\nabla^2\vec{V} }. \]
